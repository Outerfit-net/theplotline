# Iowa Sub-Regions
*Draft: 2026-03-02*

## Overview

Iowa is the Corn Belt heartland — one of the most consistent, predictable gardening climates in North America. Glacial soil is rich and deep, moisture is adequate, growing season is moderate. Rolling prairie gives way to river bottoms. What you see is what you get: reliable, productive, not flashy.

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden in Iowa — Corn Belt heartland. Zone 4b–5b. Rolling prairie, rich deep soil, good moisture. Agricultural heartland. Consistent growing conditions.

**Local references:**
- Glacially-derived soil: the deepest, richest agricultural soil in North America
- Flat to gently rolling prairie landscape; ancient glacial deposits mean fertility
- Moisture is adequate: 35 inches rain/year, well-distributed
- Corn surrounds you: monoculture is the default landscape
- Growing season is reliably moderate: May 10 last frost, Oct 5 first frost
- Fungal diseases moderate; hail in May–June is real
- Vegetable gardening is serious business; heirloom varieties from Scandinavian/German heritage
- Conservation Reserve Program lands bring back native prairie in some areas

---

## Notes

Iowa is remarkably homogeneous for gardening purposes. One region captures the entire state. The soil quality, moisture, and growing season are consistent across regions. Elevation varies from 480 ft (Mississippi River) to 1,670 ft (Osceola County), but this doesn't create distinct gardening zones; it's all glacial prairie with reliable growing conditions.

Small variations exist (slightly drier northwest, slightly cooler northeast), but they don't justify separate sub-regions for character flavor text injection.
