# Colorado Sub-Regions
*Draft: 2026-03-02 | QA: matte_d_scry (Boulder, CO)*

Parent macro zone: `high_plains`

---

## Overview

Colorado looks like a rectangle but gardens like five different planets. The Rocky Mountains bisect the state, creating a rain shadow to the east and a desert to the west, with an alpine world in between that most of the continental US doesn't have at all. Elevation is everything — Denver (5,280 ft) and Leadville (10,152 ft) are both "Colorado" and have nothing in common.

### `co_front_range`
**Front Range Urban Corridor**
*Denver, Aurora, Lakewood, Thornton, Westminster, Arvada*
Elevation: 5,000–5,500 ft | Zone: 5b–6a | Last frost: May 7 | First frost: Oct 7

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden on Colorado's Front Range — the urban corridor where the Great Plains slam into the Rockies. Your soil is Denver blue clay: heavy, alkaline (pH 7.5–8.0), dense as concrete when dry and sticky as glue when wet. You get 14 inches of rain a year, mostly as violent afternoon thunderstorms in June and July. Hail is not a metaphor — it destroyed your tomatoes in 2023 and will again. Chinook winds in February trick the fruit trees into blooming three weeks early. Your growing season is 155 days if you're lucky. You amend obsessively, water efficiently, and stake everything.

**Local references:**
- Denver blue clay, alkaline soil, soil pH 7.5–8.0
- Hail season (May–August), softball-sized hail real possibility
- Chinook winds (warm downslope winter winds that fool plants)
- 300 sunny days/year — UV is brutal, plants sunscald
- Japanese beetles arrived 2020, still spreading
- State flower: Blue columbine (actually grows here)
- Hot crops: tomatoes struggle above 95°F — choose early varieties (Early Girl, Stupice)
- Xeriscape culture strong — water restrictions common in drought years

### `co_boulder_foothills`
**Boulder & the Foothills**
*Boulder, Longmont, Loveland, Fort Collins, Golden, Lyons*
Elevation: 5,000–6,500 ft | Zone: 5a–5b | Last frost: May 5 | First frost: Oct 5

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden in Boulder County — where the mountains begin and the plains end, and the soil changes every half-mile. You might have river bottom loam, clay bench soil, or decomposed granite depending on which side of the hill you're on. The west wind is constant and drying. Spring comes fast and mean: 70°F one day, blizzard the next, then 75°F again. You've learned to wait. Last frost is officially May 5 but you don't trust it until Mother's Day. Fire risk has changed what you plant on the hillsides. The mountains are right there — you can see them from your garden, which means the weather comes from them.

**Local references:**
- West-facing vs east-facing slope matters enormously
- Wildfire smoke now a June–August reality; affects pollinators
- Strong wind desiccates soil fast — mulch is survival
- Mountain pine beetle changed the forest canopy above, changes water runoff
- Old Apple orchards in Left Hand Canyon, Hygiene area
- Boulder Valley clay vs creek-bottom loam — subscriber location within county matters
- Elders remember when you could reliably plant Memorial Day weekend

### `co_true_plains`
**Pueblo & the Eastern Plains**
*Pueblo, Rocky Ford, La Junta, Lamar, Sterling, Las Animas*
Elevation: 4,000–5,500 ft | Zone: 6a–6b | Last frost: Apr 20 | First frost: Oct 15

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden on Colorado's plains — Pueblo south through Rocky Ford, La Junta, and out to Lamar. This is where the Front Range runs out and the sky takes over. Pueblo is the hinge: hotter than Denver, drier, more wind, and the mountains are behind you now rather than next door. Pueblo green chiles are a point of fierce local pride — different from Hatch, don't let anyone tell you otherwise. Rocky Ford cantaloupes and watermelons are famous for a reason: alkaline soil, brutal sun, and hot nights concentrate the sugar like nowhere else. Wind is your constant adversary. Spring comes 2–3 weeks earlier than Denver. Summer heat is your limiting factor, not frost.

**Local references:**
- Rocky Ford cantaloupes and watermelons — real local pride
- Pueblo chiles (different from Hatch, locals will tell you)
- Arkansas River corridor — irrigation agriculture, cottonwood groves
- Wind is relentless — windbreaks are traditional, still necessary
- Grasshoppers in drought years — serious pest, not theoretical
- Earlier spring than Front Range — plant 2–3 weeks ahead of Denver
- Soil often sandier than Denver clay — better drainage, faster warmup

### `co_western_slope`
**Western Slope & Grand Valley**
*Grand Junction, Montrose, Delta, Fruita, Palisade, Glenwood Springs*
Elevation: 4,500–6,000 ft | Zone: 6b–7a | Last frost: Apr 15 | First frost: Oct 20

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden on Colorado's Western Slope — the other side of the Rockies, in the rain shadow of everything. Grand Junction gets 8 inches of rain a year. Irrigation is not a technique here, it's the only reason anything grows. The Grand Valley is peach and wine grape country — Palisade peaches are famous for a reason, the combination of volcanic soil, intense sun, and cool nights produces fruit of absurd quality. Your soil is often sandy loam over clay, alkaline, low in organic matter. Summer is hot and dry. Spring comes earlier than anywhere else in Colorado. Winter is mild enough that you sometimes get confused into planting too soon.

**Local references:**
- Palisade peaches — the pride of the Western Slope
- Grand Valley wine region (Bookcliff, Plum Creek, others)
- Book Cliffs and Colorado National Monument as landscape context
- Colorado River irrigation — water rights culture is real
- Invasive tamarisk along river corridors
- Peach borer, codling moth — tree fruit pests are the main battle
- Desert varnish soils, caliche layer common — breaks hardpan or plants fail
- First area in CO to plant in spring, first to harvest

### `co_mountain`
**Mountain Communities**
*Telluride, Aspen, Steamboat Springs, Breckenridge, Crested Butte, Durango (upper), Leadville*
Elevation: 7,000–10,000 ft | Zone: 3b–5a | Last frost: Jun 15 | First frost: Sep 1

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden at altitude — somewhere between 7,000 and 10,000 feet, where summer is 90 days long if you're lucky and winter is the default season. Your last frost is mid-June. Your first frost is September 1st. You are not growing tomatoes to ripeness outside; you are growing them in a greenhouse or you are not growing them. What you can grow — cold-tolerant brassicas, root vegetables, alpine flowers, the hardiest herbs — you grow with ferocious intensity because the season is short and the light is extraordinary. UV radiation at altitude is intense. Soil is often rocky, acidic, low in organic matter, and cold: it doesn't warm up until July. Elk eat everything. You have made peace with this or you have built an 8-foot fence.

**Local references:**
- Elk pressure — real and constant, not decorative
- Short-season varieties only: Stupice tomato, Astia pepper, fast-maturing brassicas
- Season extension is survival: cold frames, row covers, low tunnels
- Acidic soil from conifer duff — pH 5.5–6.5, very different from Front Range
- Snow in any month — June snow not uncommon, August snow not unheard of
- Intense afternoon sun + cool nights = extraordinary flavor concentration
- Community gardens at altitude are gathering places (Crested Butte, Steamboat)
- Aspen root systems come up through gardens — battle is real

### `co_san_luis`
**San Luis Valley**
*Alamosa, Monte Vista, Antonito, Center*
Elevation: 7,500 ft | Zone: 4b | Last frost: May 25 | First frost: Sep 15

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden in the San Luis Valley — a high-altitude flatland ringed by mountains, one of the coldest inhabited places in the contiguous US. Alamosa holds the record for largest temperature swing in a single day. You get 7 inches of rain a year but you have artesian wells — water is here, it's just underground. The valley grows potatoes at industrial scale because that's what thrives here: cold-tolerant, underground, patient. Your frost-free window is 90–110 days. Summer days are warm and brilliant; summer nights drop to 40°F. Everything you grow is adapted to cold, drought, and altitude simultaneously. The Great Sand Dunes are visible from some gardens. That kind of place.

**Local references:**
- San Luis Valley potatoes — major commercial crop, locals eat them all winter
- Artesian aquifer — well water available but rights complex
- Great Sand Dunes National Park as geographic anchor
- Cold nights even in summer — great for cool-season crops year-round
- Unique microclimate: surrounded by peaks on all sides, creates weather inversions
- Potato beetle is the main pest; Colorado potato beetle named for here
- Short season forces intense focus on efficiency
