# Texas Sub-Regions
*Draft: 2026-03-02*

Parent macro zone: `south_central`

---

## Overview

Texas is nine different gardening worlds stacked together. From the piney woods of East Texas to the limestone hills of Hill Country to the Rio Grande Valley's tropical heat, elevation and rainfall drop from east to west, temperatures rise from north to south, and local pride runs hot everywhere. A Texas gardener in Houston has almost nothing in common with one in El Paso.

## Sub-Regions

### `tx_panhandle`
**Texas Panhandle**
*Amarillo, Lubbock, Plainview*
Elevation: 3,500–4,500 ft | Zone: 7b–8a | Last frost: May 1 | First frost: Oct 15

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden in the Texas Panhandle — Amarillo, Lubbock, the high plains. Zone 7b–8a. Short growing season, intense sun, wind that never stops. Hail season is real. The soil is caliche-heavy, water-restrictive; hardpan is typical. Winter lows hit -15°F.

**Local references:**
- Caliche layer (calcium carbonate hardpan) is the main battle — it blocks roots and water
- Wind breaks are essential — traditional windbreak design (mulberry, cottonwood rows) still used
- Hail is real and devastating; crop insurance is a thing; May–July hail season
- Short season forces fast-maturing varieties
- Irrigation water quality varies (some wells are saline); check water before planting
- Dust storms in spring; high wind always
- Winter can hit -15°F; spring is unpredictable (warm spell then freeze)
- Dryland farming legacy; most gardeners irrigate

### `tx_north_central`
**North Central Texas (Dallas-Fort Worth)**
*Dallas, Fort Worth, Arlington, Plano*
Elevation: 400–600 ft | Zone: 8a | Last frost: Apr 10 | First frost: Nov 1

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden in North Central Texas — Dallas, Fort Worth, the metroplex. Zone 8a. Hot humid summers, mild winters, hail and severe thunderstorms. Red clay soil, hard and tight. Spring arrives early, summer arrives hard. Oak wilt is a threat to shade trees.

**Local references:**
- Red clay soil (tight, hard, pH 7.5–8.0) needs heavy amendment; clay-breaking compost is mandatory
- Oak wilt fungus is a serious concern; live oaks are culturally important and vulnerable
- Hot humid summers (100°F+ July–August, 80%+ humidity) stress plants
- Severe thunderstorms with hail (May–June) are annual
- Spring is fast — plants wake up early, then get hit by freezes
- Urban heat island effect in metroplex areas raises temps 5–10°F over suburbs
- Japanese beetles, fire blight on fruit trees, fire ants
- Four-season gardening possible but summer heat is brutal

### `tx_hill_country`
**Hill Country**
*Austin, San Antonio, New Braunfels, Wimberley*
Elevation: 800–1,500 ft | Zone: 8b | Last frost: Mar 15 | First frost: Nov 15

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden in the Hill Country — Austin, San Antonio. Zone 8b. Limestone hills, challenging soil, incredibly variable terrain. Spring wildflowers define the season. Summer heat hits 100°F regularly. Your soil pH is often 8.0 or higher. Soil varies radically within miles.

**Local references:**
- Limestone bedrock everywhere; pH 7.8–8.2; local saying: "Rock, not soil"
- Spring wildflower display (bluebonnets, Indian paintbrush) define the season; local pride is intense
- Water is precious; drought is cultural memory; underground springs in some areas
- Hill terrain creates microclimates — north-facing slopes are cooler and moister
- Edwards Plateau ecology; live oak, Texas privet, cedar elm are native framework
- Soil depth varies wildly: some places rock is 6 inches down, others have deep soil
- Cedar fever (juniper allergies) is real; some move out because of it
- Summer heat 100°F+, dry air (25 inches rain/year average), but occasional floods

### `tx_south_central`
**South Central Texas (San Antonio Transitional)**
*San Antonio, New Braunfels, Boerne*
Elevation: 400–1,200 ft | Zone: 8b–9a | Last frost: Mar 1 | First frost: Nov 20

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden in South Central Texas — San Antonio area transitional zone. Zone 8b–9a. Hot dry summers, mild winters, limestone hills mixed with flatter terrain. The Balcones Escarpment defines the region. Fire danger in drought years.

**Local references:**
- Transitional zone between Hill Country and Gulf Coast — variable terrain
- Balcones Escarpment is geographic spine; north of it is Hill Country, south is Gulf Coastal Plain
- Earlier spring than Hill Country; longer growing season
- Slightly more humid and rain than Hill Country
- Water (lack of it) is the theme: irrigation essential, water rights matter
- Some old Spanish irrigation acequia systems still function
- Fire danger in drought years: July–August dryness plus heat

### `tx_piney_woods`
**East Texas Piney Woods**
*Nacogdoches, Lufkin, Huntsville, Palestine*
Elevation: 100–500 ft | Zone: 8a–8b | Last frost: Mar 20 | First frost: Nov 10

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden in East Texas Piney Woods — Nacogdoches, Lufkin, deep forest. Zone 8a–8b. Acidic sandy soil, pine trees everywhere, humid summers, mild winters. It feels like the Deep South because it is. Water availability is good.

**Local references:**
- Acidic sandy loam soil (pH 5.5–6.5); pine duff creates naturally acidic conditions
- Piney landscape: long-leaf pine, loblolly pine, native understory
- Humidity and rain (45 inches/year) — more like Deep South than rest of Texas
- Sandy soil drains well but holds less water; organic matter quickly decomposes
- Water table is high in some areas — drainage can be an issue
- Deer and wild hogs are serious garden threats
- Forest shade affects planning; clearing vs. shade gardening decision
- Similar to Louisiana/Mississippi but Texas pine flavor

### `tx_gulf_coast`
**Texas Gulf Coast**
*Houston, Beaumont, Galveston, Port Arthur*
Elevation: 0–50 ft | Zone: 9a | Last frost: Feb 15 | First frost: Dec 1

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden on the Texas Gulf Coast — Houston, Beaumont, coastal humid subtropical. Zone 9a. Ninety-five inches of rain a year, tropical feel, salt spray in exposed areas. Humidity breeds fungal diseases. Summer feels like 110°F from June through September.

**Local references:**
- 95+ inches of rain/year (more than any other Texas region); flooding is real risk
- Humidity is constant (75%+ year-round); mold, mildew, fungal disease are the battles
- Sandy loam or clay loam soil, depending on location; peat in some areas
- Summer heat + humidity = 110°F heat index regularly; plants wilt in afternoon
- Tropical plants are possible (hibiscus, bougainvillea, fragrant jasmine)
- Salt spray in coastal areas; wind is often humid and corrosive
- Freeze protection year-round peace: frost is rare but happens (1985, 2021 freezes)
- Hurricane season (June–November) is real; storm surge and wind damage, then waterlogging

### `tx_rio_grande_valley`
**Rio Grande Valley**
*McAllen, Brownsville, Pharr, Mission*
Elevation: 0–100 ft | Zone: 9b–10a | Last frost: Jan 15 | First frost: Dec 20

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden in the Rio Grande Valley — McAllen, Brownsville, southernmost Texas. Zone 9b–10a. No frost some years. Tropical, humid, subtropical fruit paradise. Sugarcane, citrus, avocados. Hurricane season is real. Salinity in some soils.

**Local references:**
- Southernmost Texas: frost is rare, sometimes zero in mild winters
- Subtropical fruit country: citrus, sugarcane, avocados thrive; commercial agriculture dominates
- Rio Grande irrigation system — water rights, access, quality all matter
- Tropical plants year-round possible (mango, papaya, guava)
- Salinity in some soils (especially near coast); salt drift from ocean spray
- Hurricane season is serious; storm surge threatens coastal areas
- Warm enough year-round to grow tropical ornamentals
- Growing season is longest in Texas; essentially two seasons (growing and brief cool)

### `tx_coastal_bend`
**Coastal Bend**
*Corpus Christi, Kingsville, Robstown*
Elevation: 0–100 ft | Zone: 9a–9b | Last frost: Feb 1 | First frost: Dec 15

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden in the Coastal Bend — Corpus Christi, Kingsville. Zone 9a–9b. Salt spray in coastal areas, humid subtropical. Cattle ranching defines the landscape. Water can be saline in some areas.

**Local references:**
- Coastal salt spray affects inland gardens; live oak and mesquite are salt-tolerant native frame
- Sandy or clay loam soils; some areas have saline groundwater
- Hurricane alley — annual threat
- Cattle ranching country — wide open spaces, sparse vegetation in many areas
- Wind is constant (Gulf breezes); salt spray in coastal areas
- Warmer than Houston but less rain; transitional between Gulf Coast and Valley

### `tx_trans_pecos`
**Trans-Pecos (Far West)**
*El Paso, Van Horn, Marfa, Alpine*
Elevation: 2,500–5,000 ft | Zone: 8a | Last frost: Apr 1 | First frost: Oct 15

**Flavor text for character prompts:**
> You garden in the Trans-Pecos — El Paso, far west Texas. Zone 8a. High desert, low rain, intense sun, wind. Elevation near 4,000 feet. Soil is alkaline and poor. It's more like Arizona than the rest of Texas.

**Local references:**
- High desert east of New Mexico; about as far from sea level and humidity as Texas gets
- Rain: 8 inches/year in El Paso area; irrigation is the only gardening method
- Alkaline caliche-heavy soil; deep-rooted native plants adapted to these conditions
- Wind is relentless; dust storms happen
- Extreme temperatures possible (105°F+ summers, -10°F winters in north areas)
- More in common with Arizona desert than Texas coast
- Water rights and well quality are central concerns
- Intense solar radiation at elevation; UV is brutal
